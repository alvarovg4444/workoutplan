Routine Info

1. Key words you’ll see

WordWhat it really means
Rep (repetition)One up-and-down of the weight.
SetA bunch of reps done together before you rest.
RPE 7–8“I could have done 2–3 more reps if I had to.” A built-in safety check so you don’t go to failure every time.
1 RMThe heaviest weight you could lift once, with good form. (You don’t have to test it right now.)

2. Weekly schedule (pick the days that suit you)

DayWhat you’ll work onExample calendar
Day 1Squat + pushing muscles (chest, shoulders)Monday
Day 2Bench press + pulling muscles (back)Wednesday
Day 3Deadlift + extra legs / armsFriday

Leave one day off between Day 1 and Day 3 so your legs recover.

3. What to do each day

Day 1 – “Squat day”

  1. Back-squat – 5 sets × 3 reps
    Pick a weight you can do 3 solid reps while still feeling you could do 2 more (RPE 7–8).
  2. Paused bench press – 4 × 5
    Lower the bar, pause for 1 sec on the chest, press up.
  3. Romanian deadlift – 3 × 6
    Hip-hinge that works hamstrings and lower back.
  4. Dumb-bell split-squat – 3 × 8 per leg
  5. Incline dumb-bell press – 3 × 10
  6. 10 min quick conditioning – sled pushes, bike sprints, or brisk walking on an incline.

Day 2 – “Bench-press day”

  1. Bench press (normal pause) – 5 × 3
  2. Barbell or Pendlay row – 4 × 6
  3. Overhead press – 3 × 5
  4. Weighted pull-ups – 4 × 5
    (If you can’t do pull-ups yet, use a resistance band or do lat pull-downs.)
  5. Rear-delt fly – 3 × 12
  6. Core superset – ab-wheel roll-outs & hanging knee-raises, 3 × 10 each

Day 3 – “Deadlift day”

  1. Deadlift – 5 sets × 2 reps
  2. Front squat – 4 × 4
  3. Close-grip bench or dips – 3 × 6–8
  4. Leg press (or hack squat) – 3 × 10
  5. Arm superset – EZ-bar curls & triceps push-downs, 3 × 12 each
  6. 10 min finisher – farmer carries or kettlebell swings (great for grip and overall fitness)

4. How to warm up

  1. 5–7 min easy cardio (rower, bike, brisk walk).
  2. Joint prep: arm circles, leg swings, body-weight squats.
  3. Ramp-up sets: Start each main lift with the empty bar, then add weight in small jumps of 20 – 30 % until you reach your working weight.
    (Example for a 100 kg squat goal: bar × 10 → 40 kg × 5 → 60 kg × 3 → 80 kg × 1 → work sets at 100 kg.)

5. How and when to add weight

First 8–12 weeks (simple “every-time” rule)

  • If you finished all the sets and the last set still felt like RPE 8 or easier, add weight next week.
  • Lower-body lifts: +5 kg.
  • Upper-body lifts: +2–2.5 kg (or the next small plate you own).

You’ll probably increase the weight each session for 4–5 weeks, then every second session as it gets heavier.

After that (slightly more advanced “4-week wave”)

  1. Week 1: Use ~75 % of your best lift (feels light).
  2. Week 2: 80 %.
  3. Week 3: 85 % (hard week).
  4. Week 4: 60–65 % (easy “deload” to recover).

Then repeat, a little heavier than last time. If you don’t know your true 1 RM, no problem—just keep the “add weight when it feels doable” rule for your first few months.

6. Eating, sleeping, and feeling good

HabitAim for
Sleep7–9 h each night (muscles repair while you snooze).
ProteinAbout 2 g per kg of body-weight daily (e.g., 150 g if you weigh 75 kg).
CaloriesA small daily surplus (≈ 200–300 kcal above maintenance) helps strength climb.
Hydration2–3 L water a day.
Soreness checkA little muscle ache is fine; sharp joint pain is not. If something hurts in a bad way, stop and ask for help.

7. Tracking progress (super simple)

What to noteHow
Weights and repsSnap a photo of the workout log on your phone or jot it in a notebook.
RPE of last setWrite a number 6–10 beside it (10 = absolute max).
Body-weight (optional)Weigh once a week, same time of day.

If the numbers in your log are creeping up over the months, the program is working. 🎉

Bottom line

  1. Show up three times a week, hit the big lifts first, do the support work, go home.
  2. Add a little weight whenever the current load feels “pretty hard but not a grinder.”
  3. Eat, sleep, and repeat—strength is just patient practice stacked over many weeks.
